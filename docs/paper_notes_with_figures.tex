\begin{abstract}
We introduce a path-statistical diagnostic for graph discretizations of continuum geometries, based on the anisotropy of shortest-path multiplicities on graph-distance shells. Given a graph $G$, a center vertex $p$, and a graph radius $r_g$, we count the number of distinct shortest paths from $p$ to vertices on the shell $S_{r_g}(p)$ and define a logarithmic shell-anisotropy estimator $C_{\log}(p,r_g)$. We test this estimator on graph discretizations of a Flamm paraboloid, interpreted as a spatial Schwarzschild benchmark, and compare its radial organization with the Schwarzschild Kretschmann profile $K_{\mathrm{Schw}}=48M^2/r^6$. The raw vertex-level estimator is noisy, but radial binning reveals a strong correlation between $C_{\log}$ and $\log K_{\mathrm{Schw}}$. We further introduce matched-flat controls with the same radial sampling as the Flamm graph but with the embedded height profile removed. Across random seeds and several nearest-neighbor graph constructions, the Flamm benchmark shows a stable strong radial trend that is not reproduced by matched-flat controls. The estimator is not proposed as a discrete Kretschmann scalar, but as a curvature-sensitive graph diagnostic complementary to existing graph-curvature constructions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Curvature is one of the central geometric quantities in differential geometry and general relativity. In smooth Riemannian or Lorentzian geometry, curvature is encoded in tensorial objects such as the Riemann tensor, Ricci tensor, scalar curvature, and curvature invariants. For example, in the Schwarzschild spacetime the Kretschmann scalar is

\begin{equation}
K_{\mathrm{Schw}} = \frac{48M^2}{r^6}.
\end{equation}

The question of how to define useful analogues of curvature in discrete structures has several motivations, including network science, manifold learning, and discrete approaches to spacetime. There is already a substantial literature on graph curvature, especially Ricci-type notions such as Ollivier-Ricci curvature and Forman-Ricci curvature \cite{Ollivier2009,Forman2003,Samal2018}. There are also convergence results showing that suitably generalized Ollivier curvature on random geometric graphs can recover Ricci curvature in the continuum limit \cite{VanDerHoorn2021}. In discrete spacetime settings, causal-set constructions have been used to define scalar curvature operators and higher-curvature observables \cite{BenincasaDowker2010,DeBrito2023}. More recently, path-count observables on Hasse diagrams have also been explored as curvature-sensitive quantities in causal sets \cite{Alfyorov2026}.

The present work follows a different but related direction. Instead of attempting to define a Ricci curvature on edges, or a tensorial curvature invariant from a discrete differential operator, we ask whether the multiplicity of shortest paths across graph-distance shells contains a curvature-like signal. The motivation comes from the intuition that curvature affects the spreading and focusing of geodesics. In a graph, the closest analogue of geodesic multiplicity is the number of shortest paths between vertices. Therefore, anisotropy in shortest-path counts across a shell may encode information about the local geometric organization of the graph.

This idea also connects to an earlier exploratory Wolfram Summer School project on possible Kretschmann-like diagnostics for hypergraphs, where discrepancies in the number of geodesic paths from a point to boundary points were proposed as a possible curvature-sensitive signal \cite{Palomino2021}. The present note reformulates that idea more cautiously and tests it in controlled graph discretizations of known geometries.

The contribution of this note is not a new rigorous discrete Kretschmann scalar. Rather, we introduce and test a shell-based path-anisotropy diagnostic, denoted $C_{\log}$, and benchmark it against the Schwarzschild radial curvature profile using a Flamm paraboloid graph. We also perform flat, matched-flat, multi-seed, and $k$-sensitivity controls to test whether the observed radial signal is robust.

\section{Relation to existing work}

Several established approaches already assign curvature-like quantities to graphs and discrete spaces. Ollivier-Ricci curvature defines curvature through contraction of probability measures under optimal transport, generalizing Ricci curvature to Markov chains and metric spaces \cite{Ollivier2009}. Forman-Ricci curvature is a combinatorial Ricci-type curvature for cell complexes, derived from a discrete Bochner-Weitzenbock viewpoint \cite{Forman2003}. Both have been used extensively in network analysis, and comparative studies show that these curvature notions capture different aspects of graph structure \cite{Samal2018}.

Another important direction concerns continuum limits. Recent work has shown that, under appropriate assumptions, Ollivier-Ricci curvature of random geometric graphs converges to the Ricci curvature of the underlying Riemannian manifold \cite{VanDerHoorn2021}. This provides an important bridge between graph curvature and smooth geometry. Other graph curvature estimators, including discrete sectional curvature constructions, have also been proposed for graphs with geometric embeddings or low metric distortion \cite{DuPlessis2023}.

In causal-set theory, curvature is approached from a different perspective. The Benincasa-Dowker construction uses causal-set d'Alembertian operators to recover scalar curvature in suitable continuum approximations \cite{BenincasaDowker2010}. Higher-curvature operators have also been studied in causal-set quantum gravity \cite{DeBrito2023}. Recent work has explored Weyl-curvature-sensitive estimators based on path-count observables computed from the Hasse diagram of a causal set \cite{Alfyorov2026}.

The estimator studied here is complementary to these approaches. It is not an Ollivier-Ricci curvature, since it does not compare probability measures on neighboring vertices. It is not a Forman-Ricci curvature, since it is not assigned to cells or derived from a discrete Bochner formula. It is also not a causal-set operator. Instead, it is a scalar diagnostic attached to a center vertex and graph radius, computed from the anisotropy of shortest-path multiplicities on graph-distance shells.

\section{Graph shells and shortest-path anisotropy}

Let $G=(V,E)$ be a finite graph, and let $d(p,q)$ denote graph distance. For a center vertex $p\in V$ and graph radius $r_g$, define the graph-distance ball

\begin{equation}
B_{r_g}(p) = \{q\in V \mid d(p,q)\le r_g\},
\end{equation}

and the graph-distance shell

\begin{equation}
S_{r_g}(p) = \{q\in V \mid d(p,q)=r_g\}.
\end{equation}

    \label{fig:shell-ball}
\end{figure}

For each shell vertex $q\in S_{r_g}(p)$, let

\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{geo}}(p,q)
\end{equation}

denote the number of distinct shortest paths from $p$ to $q$. We then define the logarithmic shell-anisotropy estimator

\begin{equation}
C_{\log}(p,r_g)
=
\mathrm{CMD}_{q\in S_{r_g}(p)}
\left[
\log\left(N_{\mathrm{geo}}(p,q)\right)
\right],
\end{equation}

where $\mathrm{CMD}$ denotes the cubic mean deviation over the shell. The logarithm reduces sensitivity to large path-count outliers, while the deviation over the shell measures how unevenly shortest paths are distributed across different directions from $p$.

The guiding hypothesis is that curvature affects the organization of shortest paths. Therefore, a shell-based anisotropy statistic may act as a curvature-sensitive observable in graph discretizations of geometric spaces.

\section{Schwarzschild/Flamm benchmark}

The main benchmark used in this note is a graph discretization of the Flamm paraboloid, interpreted as a spatial Schwarzschild slice. Vertices are sampled on the embedded surface and connected using a $k$-nearest-neighbor rule. The continuum comparison target is the Schwarzschild Kretschmann scalar,

\begin{equation}
K_{\mathrm{Schw}} = \frac{48M^2}{r^6}.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{benchmark_geometries.png}
    \caption{Benchmark point clouds used in the graph-discretization tests. The main benchmark is the Flamm/Schwarzschild spatial-slice-inspired geometry, with flat and hyperboloid-like geometries used as controls or additional test cases.}
    \label{fig:benchmark-geometries}
\end{figure}

Since $K_{\mathrm{Schw}}$ depends only on the radial coordinate $r$, we compare the graph estimator with the radial curvature profile. Raw vertex-level comparisons are noisy because the graph is randomly sampled and the estimator is local. Therefore, we also use radial binning: vertices are grouped by radial coordinate, and the mean estimator value is computed in each radial bin.

For the current runs, radial binning gives a strong correlation between the binned estimator and the logarithmic Schwarzschild curvature profile,

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Corr}(\log K_{\mathrm{Schw}}, C_{\log}) \approx 0.912.
\end{equation}

This suggests that the estimator captures the radial organization of the curvature profile more clearly than raw pointwise values.

\section{Controls}

\subsection{Flat control}

A basic flat-space control compares the global distribution of $C_{\log}$ in flat and Flamm graphs. The global distributions overlap strongly, with a Cohen effect size

\begin{equation}
d_{\mathrm{Cohen}} \approx -0.027.
\end{equation}

Thus, the relevant signal is not a simple global shift in the estimator. The diagnostic does not merely assign larger or smaller average values to curved graphs. Instead, the signal appears in the radial organization of the estimator.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{flat_vs_flamm_radial_logcmd.png}
    \caption{Flat versus Flamm radial path anisotropy. The flat control does not reproduce the strong negative radial trend seen in the Flamm/Schwarzschild benchmark.}
    \label{fig:flat-vs-flamm}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Matched-flat control}

A stronger null test uses a matched-flat control. In the Flamm benchmark, points are embedded as

\begin{equation}
(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta,z(r)).
\end{equation}

The matched-flat control keeps the same radial and angular sampling but removes the height profile,

\begin{equation}
(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta,z(r))
\mapsto
(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta).
\end{equation}

This tests whether the radial signal is caused merely by the sampling distribution. For $N=1000$, graph radius $r_g=3$, and 12 radial bins, a single matched-flat run gives

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Corr}(r,C_{\log})_{\mathrm{MatchedFlat}} \approx 0.375,
\end{equation}

whereas the Flamm benchmark gives

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Corr}(r,C_{\log})_{\mathrm{Flamm}} \approx -0.985.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{matched_flat_vs_flamm_radial_logcmd_N1000.png}
    \caption{Matched-flat versus Flamm radial path anisotropy for $N=1000$. The matched-flat control preserves the radial sampling but removes the curved embedding, and it does not reproduce the strong negative Flamm trend.}
    \label{fig:matched-flat-radial}
\end{figure}

Across five random seeds at $N=1000$, $r_g=3$, and $k=16$, the matched-flat control gives

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Corr}(r,C_{\log})_{\mathrm{MatchedFlat}} = 0.180 \pm 0.315,
\end{equation}

whereas the Flamm benchmark gives

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Corr}(r,C_{\log})_{\mathrm{Flamm}} = -0.960 \pm 0.015.
\end{equation}

The Flamm signal is therefore stable across random seeds, while the matched-flat signal is variable and not consistently negative.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$k$}{k}-sensitivity}

To test dependence on graph construction, the matched-flat and Flamm comparisons were repeated for several $k$-nearest-neighbor values. For $N=1000$, $r_g=3$, 12 radial bins, and three random seeds per value of $k$, the results are:

\begin{center}

\toprule
$k$ & Matched-flat mean & Matched-flat std & Flamm mean & Flamm std \\
\midrule
12 & 0.125 & 0.330 & -0.942 & 0.016 \\
14 & 0.155 & 0.510 & -0.961 & 0.027 \\
16 & 0.022 & 0.292 & -0.960 & 0.020 \\
18 & 0.165 & 0.413 & -0.965 & 0.018 \\
20 & 0.372 & 0.166 & -0.962 & 0.023 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

    \centering

Across the tested graph connectivities, the Flamm benchmark remains strongly negatively correlated with radial coordinate, while the matched-flat control remains weak or variable. This supports the interpretation that the Flamm radial signal is not an artifact of a single nearest-neighbor choice.

\section{Discussion}

The results suggest that shortest-path anisotropy on graph shells can detect curvature-like radial organization in a Flamm/Schwarzschild benchmark. The estimator is not a tensorial curvature, nor is it derived from a discrete Riemann tensor. It should therefore not be interpreted as a discrete Kretschmann scalar. However, the combination of radial binning, matched-flat controls, seed averaging, and $k$-sensitivity suggests that the signal is not a trivial sampling artifact.

The main claim of this note is therefore modest:

\begin{quote}
The logarithmic path-anisotropy estimator provides a local graph observable whose radial organization tracks the Schwarzschild curvature profile in Flamm graph benchmarks and is not reproduced by matched-flat controls.
\end{quote}

Future work should compare this diagnostic with established graph curvature measures, including Ollivier-Ricci and Forman-Ricci curvature, and test additional benchmark geometries such as spheres, hyperbolic surfaces, and causal-set-like structures.

\section{Limitations}

The present work is exploratory. The main limitations are:

\begin{itemize}
\item The estimator is path-statistical, not tensorial.
\item The estimator is not derived from a discrete Riemann tensor.
\item The current benchmark is a spatial Flamm/Schwarzschild slice, not a full Lorentzian spacetime.
\item Raw vertex-level correlations are moderate; radial binning is needed to reveal the strongest signal.
\item The continuum-limit behavior of $C_{\log}$ is not yet known.
\item Comparisons with existing graph curvature measures remain to be done.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced a shell-based shortest-path anisotropy estimator for graph discretizations and tested it on a Flamm/Schwarzschild benchmark. The estimator does not separate flat and curved graphs through a simple global mean shift. Instead, its radial organization tracks the Schwarzschild curvature profile after binning. Matched-flat controls and $k$-sensitivity tests indicate that the Flamm signal is not reproduced by the same radial sampling without the curved embedding. These results support the use of shortest-path anisotropy as a curvature-sensitive diagnostic, complementary to existing graph-curvature approaches.
